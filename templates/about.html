This is coal.

Don't be afraid, don't be scared. It won't hurt you. It's coal.

It was dug up by men and women who work and live in the electorates of those who sit opposite.

It's coal that has ensured for over a hundred years that Australia has enjoyed an energy competitive advantage, that has delivered prosperity to Australian businesses [...]

Scott Morrison, addressing the Australian Parliament as Treasurer in 2017

After yet another record-breaking summer of heat and drought, the 2019 Australian election was set to be fought on climate change and the environment.

The environment lost.

It was Scott Morrison – not the progressive opposition – who would go on to preside over the most devastating bushfires the country has ever seen.

The Black Summer destroyed 3,000 homes,1 killed 800 million animals and choked the country in smoke for months.

For many Australians, coal is a job and a livelihood. It's the country's second largest export after iron ore.

Shortly after their election victory the government finally approved plans for a new Adani mine, after years of environmental hold-ups and activist protest. It's set to be one of Australia's biggest.

The cruel irony is that working people are the ones who will suffer most in the coming century from extreme weather and water scarcity.

It's easy to forget that much of the prosperity we enjoy is powered by billions of tonnes of fossilised material dug out of the ground.

We don't encounter it in our day-to-day lives when we flick a switch or boil a kettle.

This project started as an act of curiosity: opening a map to see where this stuff comes from, what it looks like.

Assembled here are satellite images of 115 operational coal mines in Australia.

I was inspired to present the data in this format by Josh Begley's Prison Map. Also striking are Mishka Henner's images of cattle feedlots.

Full source code is available on Github.

The database of mines is provided by Geoscience Australia.
